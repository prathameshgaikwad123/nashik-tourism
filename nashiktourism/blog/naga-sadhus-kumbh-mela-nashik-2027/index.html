Naga Sadhus at Nashik Kumbh Mela 2027
Who Are They & When to See Them

The most powerful and mysterious figures at Kumbh Mela. A complete, respectful guide to understanding and witnessing them.

Kumbh MelaApril 20269 min read

For most visitors, the first sight of a Naga Sadhu stops them completely. Ash-covered, armed with tridents, some with matted locks several feet long, moving in processions that part crowds like water — Naga Sadhus are the defining visual of Kumbh Mela. But who they actually are, and what their presence at Kumbh means, is far less understood than their appearance. This guide explains everything — clearly and respectfully.

📅 When to See Them at Nashik

Naga Sadhus lead the Amrit Snan processions on August 2, August 31, and September 11–12, 2027. They emerge from their Akhara camps before dawn — typically between 3 AM and 6 AM — and march to Ramkund or Kushavarta Kund. This is the only time most people will see them in full regalia.

Who Are Naga Sadhus?

Naga Sadhus are initiated ascetics — renunciates who have completely abandoned worldly life to pursue moksha (liberation). The word "Naga" comes from Sanskrit meaning naked, referring to the traditional practice of living without clothing as a rejection of all worldly attachment and ego.

They belong to the Shaiva tradition (followers of Lord Shiva) and live in organised groups called Akharas — ancient monastic orders that have existed for over 1,200 years. Traditionally they were also warrior-monks, trained to protect temples and pilgrimage sites during invasions — which is why many still carry weapons like trishuls (tridents), swords and axes.

A Naga Sadhu undergoes a long and rigorous initiation process that typically spans 6–12 years. The final initiation involves a symbolic death and rebirth — the initiate performs their own funeral rites (pind daan), signifying that they have died to the world and are now beyond it. After initiation they renounce their birth name, family connections and all possessions.

What They Actually Practice — Daily Life

Away from Kumbh, Naga Sadhus live in the Himalayas, forests, or within their Akhara compounds. Their daily routine involves:

  • Dhuni — a sacred fire that is never extinguished, around which they meditate, sleep and live
  • Vibhuti — sacred ash smeared all over the body, representing Shiva's nature and the impermanence of the physical form
  • Yoga and meditation — many hours daily, often in extreme postures
  • Rudraksha — beads of the Elaeocarpus seed worn as sacred garlands
  • Cannabis (Ganja) — considered sacred to Shiva and used ritually, not recreationally, by many Naga Sadhus
  • One meal per day — typically received as bhiksha (alms) from devotees

The 13 Akharas at Nashik Kumbh Mela 2027

At Nashik Kumbh Mela 2027, 13 Akharas participate. They are divided into Shaiva (worshippers of Shiva) and Vaishnava (worshippers of Vishnu) traditions, and they bath at different ghats — Shaiva at Kushavarta Kund in Trimbakeshwar, Vaishnava at Ramkund in Nashik.

Juna Akhara

The largest Akhara with the highest number of Naga Sadhus — over 5 lakh initiated members. Most visually striking procession.

The Amrit Snan Procession — What Actually Happens

The Amrit Snan procession is not a performance. It is a sacred ritual that has occurred at every Kumbh for centuries. Understanding what happens helps you witness it correctly.

12 AM – 2 AM
Akhara camps prepare. Sadhus wake, perform rituals at the dhuni fire, apply vibhuti, dress in procession garb. Drums begin sounding within the camp walls. General public cannot enter.
3 AM – 4 AM
Procession forms and departs. Akharas move in strict seniority order. Elephants, horses, chariots carrying senior saints move at the front. Naga Sadhus march on foot. Drums, conches and chanting fill the air. The energy is extraordinary.
4 AM – 6 AM
Sacred bath. Each Akhara enters the ghat in order. Senior saints bathe first. The procession continues until all Akharas complete their snan. The collective chanting during this period is described by every witness as unlike anything else.
After 6 AM
General public bathing opens. Once all Akharas complete their snan, the ghat opens to the public. Most pilgrims bathe between 6 AM and 10 AM on Snan days.

How to Respectfully Witness Naga Sadhus at Nashik Kumbh

Naga Sadhus are not a tourist attraction. They are serious ascetics who have dedicated their entire existence to spiritual practice. Approaching them with respect is essential — and practically speaking, approaching them disrespectfully is also unwise.

  • Do not touch a Naga Sadhu without their clear invitation to do so
  • Ask before photographing — many senior sadhus do not permit photographs. Always ask with folded hands. Some do allow it; many do not.
  • Do not offer money directly — it is considered disrespectful. Offerings of fruit, flowers or sacred items are more appropriate if you wish to offer something.
  • Watch from a respectful distance during the procession — do not try to push to the front
  • Do not call out or shout at Naga Sadhus in the procession to get their attention
  • Receive blessings correctly — if a Sadhu offers a blessing, bow your head and receive it with both hands or folded hands
📷 Photography Guidance

The best position to photograph the Amrit Snan procession is from elevated ground or rooftops along the procession route, at least 2–3 hours before the procession begins. By the time the procession starts, street-level positions are completely packed. Many photographers position themselves the evening before.

Where Are the Akhara Camps in Nashik?

During Kumbh Mela, each Akhara sets up a large camp (called a Sadhugram) in designated areas of Nashik and Trimbakeshwar. The Maharashtra government assigns specific zones to each Akhara. These camps are essentially self-contained villages with kitchens, temples, medical facilities and accommodation.

Devotees are generally welcome to visit Akhara camps outside of peak times and interact respectfully with sadhus. The camps serve free meals (prasad) to thousands of visitors daily. The atmosphere inside an Akhara camp — incense, dhuni fires, kirtan, the sight of hundreds of sadhus going about their routines — is itself a profound experience separate from the Amrit Snan day.

The Difference at Nashik — Shaiva vs Vaishnava

Nashik Kumbh is unique because it has both Shaiva and Vaishnava Akharas bathing at different locations. At Prayagraj and Haridwar, they bath together. At Nashik the split is geographical:

  • Shaiva Akharas (including the Naga Sadhus of Juna, Niranjani etc.) march to Kushavarta Kund in Trimbakeshwar
  • Vaishnava Akharas (Bairagis) march to Ramkund in Nashik city

If you want to witness the most visually dramatic procession — the ash-covered Naga Sadhus — you need to be in Trimbakeshwar on September 12, 2027. The Nashik (Ramkund) procession on September 11 features Vaishnava sadhus who are clothed and equally magnificent but a different visual experience.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can anyone become a Naga Sadhu?

Traditionally yes, though the process is extremely rigorous. The initiation requires years of preparation under a guru, passing multiple tests of austerity, and the willingness to renounce all worldly attachments permanently. Women can also be initiated as Naga Sadhvis in certain Akharas.

Are Naga Sadhus dangerous?

No. They are ascetics focused entirely on spiritual practice. However they are also not to be provoked, disrespected, or treated as entertainment. Respectful interaction is always welcomed.

Do Naga Sadhus always live naked?

Most Shaiva Naga Sadhus traditionally live without clothing as a sign of complete renunciation. During Kumbh processions some wear minimal saffron cloth. In colder months or urban settings some wear basic coverings. Vaishnava Naga (Digambar) sadhus also follow similar practices.

What do Naga Sadhus eat?

They subsist primarily on bhiksha — food given freely by devotees. Most eat once a day. Their diet is simple — rice, dal, vegetables, fruit. Cannabis is used by some as a sacred ritual substance associated with Lord Shiva.
